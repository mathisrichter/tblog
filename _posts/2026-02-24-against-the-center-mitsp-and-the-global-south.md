There is a geography of prestige in contemporary theater that most of us navigate without naming it. A production that originates at the Schaubühne in Berlin, transfers to the Edinburgh International Festival, tours to BAM in New York, and arrives at the Barbican in London has made a particular journey — one that confers a specific kind of international legitimacy, that marks its makers as belonging to a recognized circuit of seriousness. Productions that originate elsewhere, that move along different routes, through different cities, into different rooms, are rarely granted the same weight. They can be extraordinary — world-changing, formally radical, urgently necessary — and they will still be treated as regional curiosities, as supplements to the main program, as evidence of a programmer's adventurousness rather than as the thing itself.

This is the system that MITsp has, since its founding in 2014, been quietly and persistently working against. The Mostra Internacional de Teatro de São Paulo does not announce itself as a corrective to European hegemony in theater programming; that kind of rhetoric tends to produce festivals that feel like they are making a point rather than making art. What MITsp does instead is simply program from the conviction that the most important work being made today is not concentrated in Europe, and then hold that conviction consistently across eleven editions. The cumulative effect has been to create something that the mainstream international circuit still lacks: a sustained, serious encounter with theater from the Global South on the Global South's own terms.

![Avenida Paulista with the MASP museum — one of São Paulo's great cultural arteries, home to the Teatro do SESI where several MITsp performances take place](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/Avenida_paulista_masp.jpg)

Consider what the 2024 edition — themed around Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the Middle East — actually contained. Gregory Maqoma and Thuthuka Sibisi's *Broken Chord* was a piece that moved between Xhosa dance tradition and the specific history of The African Choir's 1891 tour of Britain and North America, refusing both the nostalgic and the simply political in favor of something more difficult and more honest: a reckoning with what it means to perform Blackness for a white audience, across more than a century. Jaha Koo, the South Korean artist whose solo works have slowly accumulated an international following without quite breaking through to the main touring circuit, presented in São Paulo work that audiences in Berlin and London have still largely not had the chance to see. The Black Circus of the Republic of Bantu, *Wayqeycuna [My brothers]*, *Prophetics (We are already born)* — each of these pieces arrived in São Paulo carrying its own geography, its own aesthetic history, its own claims on how theater can be made and what it can do.

The difficulty, and it is a real difficulty, is that festivals like MITsp struggle to close the loop: to get the work they champion into the circuits that would give it lasting international reach. Part of this is what MITbr, MITsp's Brazilian showcase platform, is designed to address. Since 2018, MITbr has brought eighty to one hundred international programmers and curators to São Paulo each year, with the explicit aim of facilitating international touring for Brazilian work. The results have been significant — seventy national shows and forty artists or companies have circulated internationally through the platform. But the deeper problem is structural: the prestige circuits are self-reinforcing. Programmers book what other programmers have programmed. Critics review what their editors send them to review. The map of "world theater" stays more or less where it was, with minor amendments.

What MITsp offers, and what I find myself returning to as the 2026 edition approaches, is less a solution to this problem than a sustained demonstration that the problem exists — and that it does not have to. When nora chipaumire's *Dambudzo* opened at Sesc Pompeia in 2025, recreating Zimbabwean shebeens as spaces of cultural resistance and grief in the building that Lina Bo Bardi herself designed as a reclamation of working-class space, something happened in the room that I have difficulty attributing to anything other than the specificity of its placement. This was not a performance that would have meant the same thing in Vienna or Amsterdam. It meant what it meant precisely because it was happening in São Paulo, in a building that carries its own history of resistance, for an audience that arrived carrying theirs.

![São Paulo from the air — a city of contradictions that has made itself one of the world's most important stages for contemporary performance](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/Sao_Paulo_Brazil_Skyline_Aerial%2C_December_2024.jpg)

I am aware that there is something uncomfortable in a European critic celebrating a Latin American festival for the corrective it offers to European cultural dominance. The logic too easily becomes: MITsp matters because it helps us — inhabitants of the prestige circuit — see what we have been missing. But that inverts the actual relationship. MITsp does not exist to supplement the European festival calendar. It exists because São Paulo is one of the world's great cultural cities, because Brazil has a theater tradition of extraordinary richness, and because Antonio Araujo and Guilherme Marques believed — correctly — that the free international performance scene deserved a festival in the Global South that looked at the world from there, rather than from the vantage point of its traditional centers. The question is not whether the center will notice. The question is whether the work is happening, whether audiences are encountering it, and whether the conversation is being had. At MITsp, the answer to all three is yes.

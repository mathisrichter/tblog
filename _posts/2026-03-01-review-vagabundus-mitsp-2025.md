I have been thinking about *Vagabundus* for a year. When I walked out of the Teatro do SESI-SP on Avenida Paulista on the evening of March 15, 2025, after Idio Chichava's seventy-minute piece for thirteen dancers and singers from Mozambique had ended, I stood outside in the São Paulo night for longer than I usually do after performances. Not because I was processing something difficult — though the piece is not easy — but because I was reluctant to let the world of it dissolve, which it was already beginning to do, the way those things do, almost the moment you step outside the room.

*Vagabundus*, made by Chichava and the Converge+ collective, is ostensibly about migration. The word itself — from the Latin *vagabundus*, meaning to wander — sets the terms clearly enough. But what the piece actually does with migration is more complex and more risky than a thematic statement. It treats displacement not as a political condition to be illustrated but as a physical one to be inhabited: the bodies on stage are in a constant state of movement that is neither arrival nor departure but something in between, something closer to the perpetual motion of the person who has learned to carry their home inside themselves.

![A performance at the Sesc Pompeia, MITsp's main venue — the same complex that housed several of the festival's most memorable 2025 moments](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/94/C%C3%ADnica_Cia._de_Teatro_%28SESC_Pomp%C3%A9ia%2C_S%C3%A3o_Paulo%2C_SP%2C_Brasil%29._%2830011506044%29.jpg)

The choreographic language draws on Makonde ritual tradition — Chichava, who is Mozambican, has spent years researching the ceremonial dance forms of the Makonde people of northern Mozambique and southern Tanzania — but it does not present this tradition as something intact, whole, or simply passed down. Instead, the Makonde movement vocabulary appears in *Vagabundus* as something that the performers are actively remembering in their bodies: reaching for gestures that are half-present, that arrive in fragments and then recede, that are interrupted by other movement registers — contemporary dance, the repetitive rhythms of physical labor, moments of sheer stillness that feel like the pauses in a sentence. The effect is of cultural memory as a physical experience, as something that lives in the body rather than the mind and that can be lost and recovered in the body.

The sound of *Vagabundus* is as important as its movement. The thirteen performers are also singers, and the piece's score is built almost entirely from a cappella choral voice: layered, complex, shifting between unison and startling polyphony with a naturalness that suggests years of collective practice. There are moments when the singing swells to fill the entire space and others when it dwindles to a single voice, barely audible, as if the community that the chorus represents has been reduced almost to nothing. These contractions and expansions track an emotional journey that the choreography alone could not sustain. By the final twenty minutes, when the full ensemble sings together in a configuration that feels simultaneously like a ritual and like an act of collective determination, the audience in São Paulo — which included, I noticed, a significant number of members of the Brazilian-African diaspora — was visibly moved in the way that only genuine art can move people: not through manipulation but through recognition.

![The architecture of Sesc Pompeia: Lina Bo Bardi's design transformed a factory into a space of culture, a history that resonates with the thematic preoccupations of much of MITsp's programming](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3f/Arq._Lina_Bo_Bardi%2C_SESC_Pomp%C3%A9ia_%28S%C3%A3o_Paulo%2C_SP%2C_Brasil%29._%2841376970382%29.jpg)

I write about this now, a year later, in the week before MITsp 2026 opens, partly because the piece has stayed with me and partly because I think *Vagabundus* illustrates something important about what this festival is for. It is not a piece that has toured widely on the European circuit. It has not been to Avignon or Edinburgh or Wiener Festwochen. In the normal economy of international theater prestige, it might never be. But it is a piece that needed to exist, that needed to be seen, and that found its audience — in São Paulo, of all places, in that old factory redesigned by Lina Bo Bardi into one of the world's best cultural spaces. The encounter between Chichava's work and this particular city, this particular audience, at this particular festival, produced something irreplaceable. That is what MITsp, at its best, makes possible: not a corrective to the international touring circuit, but an alternative to it. One that makes its own arguments, in its own rooms, for its own reasons.

*Vagabundus* was presented at the Teatro do SESI-SP, Avenida Paulista 1313, as part of MITsp 2025.

In less than a month, the Sesc Pompeia in São Paulo's Pompeia neighborhood — that extraordinary brutalist complex conceived by Lina Bo Bardi from a reclaimed factory — will once again become the pulsing center of one of the world's most important theater festivals. The Mostra Internacional de Teatro de São Paulo, known simply as MITsp, opens on March 6 for its eleventh edition, and it arrives with the weight of a decade's worth of conviction behind it: that the most vital, necessary contemporary performance is not being made at the Schaubühne or the National Theatre, but in Mozambique, Zimbabwe, South Korea, Argentina, and indeed across Brazil itself.

MITsp was founded in 2014 by Antonio Araujo — artistic director of Teatro da Vertigem and professor at the University of São Paulo — and Guilherme Marques, cultural producer and director of the International Theater Center CIT-Ecum. Their ambition was explicit: to fill the vacuum left by the historic international festivals once curated by Ruth Escobar, which had long been a vital artery connecting Brazilian theater practitioners to the world. In a decade, MITsp has presented 198 productions from 49 countries to more than 215,000 spectators, and it has done so with a curatorial intelligence that has made it essential reading for programmers and critics who care about where theater is actually going.

![The Sesc Pompeia, MITsp's primary venue, a former factory transformed by architect Lina Bo Bardi](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a3/Fachada_norte_do_Sesc_Pompeia.jpg)

What distinguishes MITsp from the European festival circuit — from Avignon, from the Holland Festival, from Wiener Festwochen — is a curatorial commitment to geographic and political redress. This is not merely a tokenistic gesture toward diversity. The 2024 edition, themed around Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the Middle East, was a deliberate intervention against what its curators identified as the structural Eurocentrism of the international touring circuit. The results were revelatory: Gregory Maqoma and Thuthuka Sibisi's *Broken Chord* reimagined the 1891 journey of The African Choir through Xhosa dance and contemporary choreography, while the unlikely collaboration between Carmen Luz (Brazil) and Jaha Koo (South Korea) interrogated the very category of "Western theatre" from within.

The 2025 tenth anniversary edition deepened this commitment. The festival honored a constellation of emblematic Brazilian theater figures — Abdias do Nascimento, Cacilda Becker, José Celso Martinez Corrêa, Ruth Escobar — while presenting some of the most formally adventurous performances I have seen in years. Idio Chichava's *Vagabundus*, which brought 13 dancers and singers from Mozambique in a piece threading migration, Makonde ritual, and a cappella choral traditions, left audiences in the Teatro do SESI-SP struck silent. Nora chipaumire's *Dambudzo*, an immersive multimedia installation at Sesc Pompeia that recreated Zimbabwean shebeens as sites of cultural memory and resistance, seemed to absorb the building's entire atmosphere into its logic.

For 2026, the eleventh edition promises to continue this trajectory. The festival's stated themes — racism, transphobia, disability, the democratization of cultural access — are not new concerns for MITsp, but they arrive with renewed urgency in a political climate that makes the festival's very existence feel like a political act. The MITbr platform, which since 2018 has connected international programmers and curators with Brazilian work, returns with five productions from five Brazilian states: Patfudyda's *Vogue Funk* from Rio de Janeiro, blending baile funk and vogue ball as political language; the Corpo de Dança do Amazonas with a work drawing from Tikuna indigenous worldview; and three further pieces from Paraná, Goiás, and Mato Grosso do Sul. If you can be in São Paulo between March 6 and 15, there is nowhere else you should be.

![The São Paulo cityscape, a sprawling megalopolis where MITsp insists on making space for the world](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Panoramic_view_of_S%C3%A3o_Paulo_City.jpg)

A word about the city itself: São Paulo is not an easy place to love from the outside. It is too large, too dense, too contradictory — a city of twenty-two million people where gleaming corporate towers and favelas share the same horizon. But for those who make the effort, who follow the festival between Sesc Pompeia and Sesc Vila Mariana, who sit in the café at the Centro Cultural São Paulo debating what they have just seen until midnight, the city reveals itself as something extraordinary: a place where culture is not a luxury but a civic necessity, where theater is genuinely debated by people who have no professional obligation to care about it. MITsp did not create this culture, but it has become one of its most vital expressions. The eleventh edition opens on March 6. Don't miss it.

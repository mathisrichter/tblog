Antonio Araujo meets me in a café near the Theatro Municipal — that ornate, impassive beaux-arts building from 1911 that has watched São Paulo transform itself around it for more than a century — three days before MITsp 2026 opens. He is wearing the slightly distracted look of someone who has seventeen things on his mind and has chosen to discuss sixteen of them simultaneously. Araujo is the artistic director of Teatro da Vertigem, one of Brazil's most internationally recognized theater companies, and since 2014 he has co-directed MITsp alongside producer Guilherme Marques. In eleven editions, the festival has presented 198 productions from 49 countries. He orders an espresso and begins talking before I have asked my first question.

---

**You've described MITsp as filling a void left by the historic festivals curated by Ruth Escobar. After eleven editions, has the void been filled, or does it look different than you expected?**

The void was never going to be filled in the way I imagined in 2014. What we discovered very quickly is that the void wasn't simply an absence of an international festival in São Paulo. It was an absence of a certain kind of conversation — a sustained conversation between Brazilian theater and the rest of the world that wasn't mediated entirely by European institutions. Ruth Escobar had that kind of reach, that kind of curatorial authority, and when she stopped, the Brazilian theater scene became more isolated in ways that were difficult to articulate but that you felt everywhere. What we've built with MITsp is something different from what she did, but it comes from the same conviction: that Brazilian theater practitioners and audiences need direct encounter with international work, and that international theater practitioners need direct encounter with Brazil.

**The festival has become known for its focus on the Global South — Africa, Latin America, Asia, the Middle East. Was that always the intention?**

Not explicitly, no. In the first two or three editions, we were programming eclectically, trying to understand what MITsp actually was and what it could be. The shift toward a more deliberate focus on the Global South happened gradually, as we became more aware of the structural problem we were responding to. The international touring circuit — the network of major festivals that determines what gets seen and what gets prestige — is built on a set of assumptions about where serious theater comes from. Those assumptions are changing, but slowly. When we made the 2024 edition with a focus on Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the Middle East, we weren't making a conceptual statement. We were responding to where the most exciting work was actually happening.

![The ornate interior of the Theatro Municipal, built in 1911 and site of the 1922 Week of Modern Art that transformed Brazilian culture](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/Interior_Teatro_Municipal_de_S%C3%A3o_Paulo.jpg)

**MITbr, the platform for Brazilian work within MITsp, seems designed to address the other side of that problem — getting Brazilian work out into the world rather than just bringing the world in.**

Yes, and it has worked better than I dared hope. The basic idea was simple: if you bring international programmers and curators to São Paulo and show them what's happening in Brazilian theater, some of them will want to take that work home. Since 2018 we've had between eighty and one hundred international programmers at each edition of MITbr, and we've seen seventy shows and forty artists or companies circulate internationally as a result. But the deeper effect is harder to measure: when a programmer from Germany or Japan or Morocco spends ten days in São Paulo experiencing not just the performances but the city, the conversations, the culture, their understanding of what Brazilian theater is changes in ways that affect every programming decision they make afterward. That's worth more than any single booking.

**What does the Theatro Municipal, where some MITsp events take place, represent for you in the context of the festival?**

It's a complicated building. It was built in 1911 as a monument to a certain idea of Brazilian cultural aspiration — a beaux-arts opera house that made a point about São Paulo's European ambitions. The famous Week of Modern Art happened here in 1922, which was the moment when Brazilian modernism declared its independence from exactly those European models. So the building has this double history: of imitation and then of radical departure from imitation. When we use the Cúpula space inside the Municipal for chamber performances — as we did with Guillermo Cacace's *Seagull* in 2025 — there's always a resonance with that history. A work that reimagines Chekhov through five Argentine actresses around a table, in a space that was built to stage European opera in São Paulo: the irony is productive.

![The Theatro Municipal de São Paulo, an emblem of the city's long history of cultural ambition and contradiction](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/2-Theatro-Municipal.jpg)

**Looking at eleven editions, what has surprised you most?**

The audiences. I knew São Paulo had a theater public — I've been working here my whole career, I know that. But the appetite for international contemporary performance, for work that is formally experimental or politically uncomfortable or that requires real engagement from the audience, has exceeded anything I anticipated. MITsp audiences don't want to be comforted. They come ready to encounter something that might challenge everything they expect from an evening in the theater, and when a piece delivers that, the responses are extraordinary. After Idio Chichava's *Vagabundus* last year — 13 dancers and singers from Mozambique, a seventy-minute piece that asks everything from the audience — people didn't leave. They stood in the foyer for an hour, talking to each other, to the artists, to strangers. That kind of response doesn't happen by accident. It happens because you have a public that has been cultivated over years, that has learned through encounter after encounter what contemporary performance can do.

**And what are you hoping for from the eleventh edition?**

I hope to be surprised. That's always what I hope for. We program carefully — we think hard about what each work is doing, what it says in conversation with the other works, what it says to São Paulo specifically. But theater is live, and the encounter between a performance and an audience on a particular night in a particular city always produces something that no amount of programming can predict. The best moments in ten years of MITsp have been moments of that kind: when something happened in the room that nobody planned, that the artists didn't expect, that the audience didn't expect. That's what we're here for. Everything else is preparation.
